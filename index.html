Research Question

How do US political and COVID memes differ in how they assign blame, victimhood, and heroism — and do visual, textual, and acoustic features reinforce those framings?

Summary

Memes are not trivial internet content. They assign blame, invert the emotional tone of political speech, and circulate ideological positions in a format designed for rapid sharing. This project treats them as genuine cultural artifacts and applies a full multimodal pipeline across three datasets and eight steps of analysis to find out what they are actually doing.

The most consistent finding is that Donald Trump dominates the meme data more than any other figure by a wide margin, appearing simultaneously as the top villain, hero, and victim depending on who made the meme. COVID memes are emotionally flat and tightly clustered around neutrality, with no equivalent celebratory cluster to match the partisan wins captured in US Politics memes. Moving into the campaign speech data, every politician speaks more positively than the memes about them suggest. Meme culture is actively inverting the tone of real political speech and redirecting it as blame. The COVID keyword analysis draws a direct line from Trump’s China-framing rhetoric in his 2020 speeches to the meme villain data, where China is the second most common villain tag. Finally, acoustic feature analysis on the actual debate audio shows that Trump and Biden sound genuinely different at the signal level, and that the feature separating them most clearly(zero crossing rate) also tracks with the villain count gap in memes.

Datasets

  • Memes Images: OCR Data — 5,552 political and COVID memes with OCR text and entity tags (yogesh239, Kaggle)
  • 2020 US Presidential Election Campaign Speeches — 1,081 cleaned speeches from Trump, Biden, Pence, and Harris (Chalkiadakis et al., 2025)
  • M-Arg Multimodal Argumentation Dataset — 17 MP3 debate clips with force-aligned utterance timestamps (Mestre et al., 2021)
